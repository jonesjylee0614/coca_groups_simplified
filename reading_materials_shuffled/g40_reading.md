## Part 1. Story (英文原文)

Dr. James stood in the **plain** /pleɪn/ (adj. 简朴的；平原) building that now served as a hospital. The **stress** /stres/ (n. 压力；强调) of his work was visible on his face. Years of **fighting** /ˈfaɪtɪŋ/ (n./v. 战斗) and **bombing** /ˈbɑːmɪŋ/ (n. 轰炸) had left this city in ruins, and he had come as a **doctor** /ˈdɑːktər/ (n. 医生) to help rebuild not just bodies but hope. **Unlike** /ʌnˈlaɪk/ (prep./adj. 不像；不同的) the chaos of war, healing required patience and **rules** /ruːlz/ (n. 规则).

In the hospital **yard** /jɑːrd/ (n. 院子), children played **despite** /dɪˈspaɪt/ (prep. 尽管) everything they had lost. One boy, wearing a **medal** /ˈmedl/ (n. 奖章) his father had earned as a **fighter** /ˈfaɪtər/ (n. 战士；拳击手), sat alone looking **sad** /sæd/ (adj. 悲伤的). His name was **Marcus** /ˈmɑːrkəs/ (n. 马库斯—男性名字), and he was only eight. James approached him slowly. "That's a beautiful **medal**," he said. The boy looked up, his eyes holding a **complexity** /kəmˈpleksəti/ (n. 复杂性) no child should know.

"My father was a **male** /meɪl/ (adj./n. 男性的) **fighter**," Marcus said. "He died in the **bombing**. They say he had **power** /ˈpaʊər/ (n. 权力；力量), but what **power** does anyone have against bombs?" James sat down beside him, the **loud** /laʊd/ (adj. 大声的) sounds of reconstruction work filling the air. He wanted to find the **right** /raɪt/ (adj. 正确的) words but knew there were none that could undo such **loss** /lɔːs/ (n. 损失).

"**Till** /tɪl/ (prep./conj. 直到) **Christmas** /ˈkrɪsməs/ (n. 圣诞节)," James began, "the city wants to rebuild the **core** /kɔːr/ (n. 核心；中心) of the town **square** /skwer/ (n. 广场). They need people to help. Would you like to be part of the **replacement** /rɪˈpleɪsmənt/ (n. 替换；替代) of what was lost?" Marcus looked uncertain. "I'm just a kid. I have **no** /noʊ/ (det. 没有) **power**."

"**Power** /ˈpaʊər/ comes in many forms," James said. "Right now, the **core** need isn't **fighting**. It's building. Your father's **membership** /ˈmembərʃɪp/ (n. 会员资格；成员身份) in this community didn't end with his death. You carry it forward." He paused. "We need to **freeze** /friːz/ (v. 冻结；冻住) the **cycle** /ˈsaɪkl/ (n. 循环) of violence and **deal** /diːl/ (v./n. 处理；交易) with building **peace** /piːs/ (n. 和平)."

Over the following weeks, Marcus worked in the **yard** alongside others. The **pension** /ˈpenʃn/ (n. 养老金；抚恤金) his mother received helped, but **their** /ðer/ (det. 他们的) **survival** /sərˈvaɪvl/ (n. 生存) depended on more than money. The town square, once a **plain** and **sad** place marked by **destruction** /dɪˈstrʌkʃn/ (n. 破坏), slowly began to change. Marcus learned to **peel** /piːl/ (v. 剥；削皮) paint from old **wood** /wʊd/ (n. 木头), preparing it for reuse. He learned that **unlike** **destroying** /dɪˈstrɔɪɪŋ/ (v. 破坏), **creating** /kriˈeɪtɪŋ/ (v. 创造) took time and **care** /ker/ (n. 关心).

One day, an **actress** /ˈæktrəs/ (n. 女演员) visited to perform for the workers. She spoke of how art could **heal** /hiːl/ (v. 治愈). "The **complexity** of **human** /ˈhjuːmən/ (adj./n. 人类的) **emotion** /ɪˈmoʊʃn/ (n. 情感) means we can hold both **sadness** /ˈsædnəs/ (n. 悲伤) and **hope** /hoʊp/ (n. 希望)," she said. Marcus realized she was **right** /raɪt/. His father's **medal** would always remind him of **loss**, but the town **square** /skwer/ they were building would remind him of **renewal** /rɪˈnuːəl/ (n. 更新).

By **Christmas**, the **square** was complete. It wasn't grand—just a **plain**, **simple** /ˈsɪmpl/ (adj. 简单的) space with **benches** /ˈbentʃɪz/ (n. 长椅). But to Marcus and others, it represented a **replacement** for **chaos** /ˈkeɪɑːs/ (n. 混乱) with **order** /ˈɔːrdər/ (n. 秩序), **fighting** with **cooperation** /koʊˌɑːpəˈreɪʃn/ (n. 合作). The **rule** of **violence** /ˈvaɪələns/ (n. 暴力) had been broken. **Till** that moment, Marcus had thought **power** meant **strength** /streŋθ/ (n. 力量). Now he knew: true **power** lies in the choice to **build** /bɪld/ (v. 建造) rather than **destroy** /dɪˈstrɔɪ/ (v. 破坏), to **deal** with **pain** /peɪn/ (n. 痛苦) through **creation** /kriˈeɪʃn/ (n. 创造) rather than more **fighting**.

## Part 2. 故事翻译 (中文)

詹姆斯医生站在现在用作医院的朴素建筑里。他脸上可见工作的压力。多年的战斗和轰炸使这座城市成为废墟，他作为医生来帮助重建的不仅是身体，还有希望。与战争的混乱不同，治愈需要耐心和规则。

在医院的院子里，孩子们尽管失去了一切仍在玩耍。一个男孩戴着他父亲作为战士赢得的奖章，独自坐着，看起来很悲伤。他的名字叫马库斯，只有八岁。詹姆斯慢慢走近他。"那是一枚漂亮的奖章，"他说。男孩抬起头，他的眼睛里有着孩子不应该知道的复杂性。

"我父亲是一名男性战士，"马库斯说。"他死于轰炸。他们说他有力量，但面对炸弹，谁有什么力量？"詹姆斯在他身边坐下，重建工作的巨大声音充满空气。他想找到正确的话，但知道没有任何话能挽回这样的损失。

"直到圣诞节，"詹姆斯开始说，"这座城市想要重建城镇广场的核心。他们需要人帮忙。你想成为替代失去之物的一部分吗？"马库斯看起来不确定。"我只是个孩子。我没有力量。"

"力量有很多形式，"詹姆斯说。"现在，核心需求不是战斗。是建设。你父亲在这个社区的成员身份并没有随着他的死亡而结束。你将它延续下去。"他停顿了一下。"我们需要冻结暴力循环，处理建设和平的问题。"

在接下来的几周里，马库斯和其他人一起在院子里工作。他母亲收到的抚恤金有帮助，但他们的生存依赖的不仅仅是钱。城镇广场曾经是一个被破坏标记的朴素而悲伤的地方，慢慢开始改变。马库斯学会了从旧木头上剥漆，准备重复使用。他了解到，与破坏不同，创造需要时间和关心。

有一天，一位女演员来为工人们表演。她谈到艺术如何能治愈。"人类情感的复杂性意味着我们可以同时怀有悲伤和希望，"她说。马库斯意识到她是对的。他父亲的奖章将永远提醒他失去，但他们正在建设的城镇广场将提醒他更新。

到圣诞节时，广场完工了。它并不宏伟——只是一个有长椅的朴素简单空间。但对马库斯和其他人来说,它代表了用秩序替代混乱,用合作替代战斗。暴力规则被打破了。直到那一刻，马库斯认为力量意味着强度。现在他知道了：真正的力量在于选择建造而不是破坏，通过创造而不是更多战斗来处理痛苦。

## Part 4. 重点句讲解

### 句子1
**原句：** "Unlike the chaos of war, healing required patience and rules."

**分析：** "Unlike"作为介词，引出对比。这句用简洁的方式对比了战争和治愈的本质差异。

**亮点：** 
- "chaos"vs."patience and rules"形成鲜明对比
- 动词"required"表明healing的必要条件，不是可选项
- 简短有力，为全文定下基调：从混乱到秩序的转变

---

### 句子2
**原句：** "They say he had power, but what power does anyone have against bombs?"

**分析：** 这是一个修辞性问句。前半句陈述他人的说法，"but"转折后用疑问句挑战这个说法。

**亮点：** 
- "power"重复使用，但意义被质疑
- 反问句比陈述句更有力——暗示答案是"没有力量"
- 体现了孩子的困惑和创伤：如果父亲有力量，为什么还会死？

---

### 句子3
**原句：** "Your father's membership in this community didn't end with his death. You carry it forward."

**分析：** 第一句用否定句式强调延续性。第二句用简短的祈使句式表达行动号召。

**亮点：** 
- "membership"将抽象的连接具体化为一种可以"carry forward"的东西
- "didn't end"vs."carry forward"强调生命的延续
- 给予孩子使命感和归属感，而非仅仅安慰

---

### 句子4
**原句：** "True power lies in the choice to build rather than destroy, to deal with pain through creation rather than more fighting."

**分析：** 这是全文主题句。使用"lies in"结构指出真正力量的所在，后接两组平行的"to do...rather than..."对比。

**亮点：** 
- "choice"强调主动性——力量来自选择，不是天生的
- 两组对比：build vs. destroy, creation vs. fighting
- "rather than more fighting"中的"more"暗示fighting已经够多了
- 完美呼应开头Marcus的问题："what power does anyone have?"——这就是答案

## Part 5. 语法聚焦 (Grammar Focus)

### 聚焦1：Unlike vs. Unlikely

**原句：** "Unlike the chaos of war, healing required patience and rules."

**聚焦：** 区分Unlike（不像）和Unlikely（不太可能）

**Unlike（介词）= 不像，与...不同**

**用法：**
- Unlike + 名词/代词
- 用于对比两个事物的不同

**例句：**
1. Unlike his brother, Tom loves sports.
2. Unlike last year, this year has been difficult.
3. Unlike coffee, tea doesn't keep me awake.

**Unlikely（形容词）= 不太可能的**

**用法：**
- It is unlikely that... = ...是不太可能的
- Unlikely to do... = 不太可能做...
- An unlikely event = 不太可能的事件

**例句：**
1. It is **unlikely** that it will rain today.
2. She is **unlikely** to change her mind.
3. This is an **unlikely** scenario.

**对比记忆：**
| 词 | 词性 | 意思 | 例句 |
|----|------|------|------|
| **Unlike** | 介词 | 不像 | Unlike you, I prefer tea. |
| **Unlikely** | 形容词 | 不可能 | It's unlikely to snow. |

**常见错误：**
❌ "Unlikely his brother, he is tall."
✅ "**Unlike** his brother, he is tall."

❌ "It is unlike that she will come."
✅ "It is **unlikely** that she will come."

---

### 聚焦2：Till vs. Until

**原句：** "Till Christmas, the city wants to rebuild the core of the town square."

**聚焦：** Till和Until的用法和区别

**基本规则：Till = Until（在大多数情况下可互换）**

**细微差别：**

1. **正式程度：**
   - **Until** 稍微更正式，更常用于书面语
   - **Till** 更口语化，但完全正确（不是缩写！）
   - **'Til** 是缩写形式，不太推荐

2. **位置使用：**
   - **Until** 可以用在句首
     - ✅ Until yesterday, I didn't know.
     - ✅ Till yesterday, I didn't know. (也可以，但不太常见)
   
   - 句中两者通用
     - Wait till/until tomorrow.
     - He worked till/until midnight.

3. **常见搭配：**
   - **Not...until** = 直到...才
     - I didn't understand until she explained.
     - 也可以用till，但until更常见

**时态使用：**
- **Till/Until + 时间点** (通常用一般现在时表将来)
  - Wait here till I return. (不是will return)
  - She'll stay until the meeting ends.

**例句对比：**
| 场景 | Till | Until |
|------|------|-------|
| 口语 | "Wait till morning!" | "Wait until morning!" |
| 书面语 | 可以 | **更常见** |
| 句首 | 较少 | **更自然** |
| Not...until | 可以 | **更常见** |

**原句分析：**
"Till Christmas" 在此使用合适，因为：
- 表达时间终点
- 口语化的对话场景
- 简洁自然

## Part 6. 延伸练习 (Practice & Reflection)

### 6.1 理解题 (Comprehension)

1. **Who** is Dr. James, and why is he in the city?
   - Dr. James is a doctor who came to help rebuild the city after years of fighting and bombing, working to restore not just bodies but hope.

2. **Why** is Marcus sitting alone in the hospital yard?
   - He is grieving his father, a fighter who died in the bombing, and struggling with feelings of powerlessness.

3. **What** does Dr. James invite Marcus to help with?
   - He invites Marcus to help rebuild the core of the town square by Christmas as part of replacing what was lost.

4. **What if** Marcus had refused to participate in rebuilding?
   - He might have remained trapped in grief and anger, never discovering that true power lies in building rather than destroying.

5. **How** does Marcus's understanding of "power" change throughout the story?
   - He initially sees power as military strength (like his father's), but learns that true power is the choice to build and create peace rather than continue cycles of violence.

   - **Answer:** complexity

   - **Answer:** Unlike

   - **Answer:** deal

### 6.3 写作任务 (Writing Prompt)

Write a short paragraph (80-100 words) about a time when you or someone you know chose to build something positive instead of focusing on anger or revenge after a difficult experience. Use at least 5 words from this group's vocabulary list.

**参考示例：**
After my uncle lost his job due to company fighting and internal stress, he could have remained sad and bitter. Unlike many who complain, he chose a different path. He used his pension to start a plain but honest business in his yard. Till today, that small replacement income has grown into something bigger. The core lesson I learned from him: true power lies not in fighting what you've lost, but in dealing with it by building something new. His membership in our family taught us all about resilience.

### 6.4 讨论问题 (Discussion)

**Question:** The story suggests that "true power lies in the choice to build rather than destroy, to deal with pain through creation rather than more fighting." Is this realistic advice for people who have experienced violence and loss? What does it take for individuals and communities to break cycles of violence?

**思考提示：**
- Consider whether choosing peace is always possible or sometimes a privilege
- Think about the difference between individual healing and community rebuilding
- Reflect on the role of justice vs. revenge in breaking violence cycles
- Discuss what resources (emotional, financial, social) are needed to "build" after destruction
- Consider the complexity of human emotion—can people hold both grief and hope?
- Think about Marcus's father's medal—should we honor fighters while promoting peace?
- Reflect on what "membership in community" means and how it extends beyond individual lives
- Consider the timeline: why does healing require patience and rules "unlike" war's chaos?
