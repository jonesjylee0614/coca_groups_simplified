## Part 1. Story (英文原文)

The **reporter** /rɪˈpɔːrtər/ (n. 记者;报告者) checked her phone one more time before entering the building. The **temperature** /ˈtemprətʃər/ (n. 温度;体温) had dropped sharply that morning, and Sarah pulled her coat tighter. She was here to **search** /sɜːrtʃ/ (v./n. 搜索;搜寻) for answers about a story that had puzzled the city for weeks.

A factory **fire** had been ruled an accident, but something felt wrong. Sarah's editor had been **highly** /ˈhaɪli/ (adv. 高度地;非常) skeptical about the official report. "Go **search** the area," he had said. "Talk to people. Find what they're not telling us." **Yet** /jet/ (adv./conj. 还;但是), as Sarah approached the burned building, she felt a wave of **uncertainty** /ʌnˈsɜːrtənti/ (n. 不确定性;不确定的事物).

Inside what remained of the factory, Sarah met Chen, a former worker. "They say the **temperature** inside reached dangerous levels that day," Chen explained. "But **normally** /ˈnɔːrmli/ (adv. 通常;正常地), we had safety systems. They should have worked."

"What do you think happened?" Sarah asked, her **preference** /ˈprefrəns/ (n. 偏爱;优先) for getting straight to the point showing in her direct question.

Chen hesitated. "I'm not **sure** /ʃʊr/ (adj. 确定的;可靠的), but I **think** there's more to this story. **Surely** /ˈʃʊrli/ (adv. 当然;必定), someone knows the truth."

Over the next few days, Sarah continued her **search**. She visited the **campus** /ˈkæmpəs/ (n. 校园;大学) where some of the factory workers had taken safety training. She talked to engineers who understood **theoretical** /ˌθiːəˈretɪkl/ (adj. 理论的;理论上的) risks. Each **encounter** /ɪnˈkaʊntər/ (n./v. 遭遇;邂逅) brought new pieces to the puzzle, but nothing definitive.

Then Sarah had an important **encounter**. At a coffee shop, she met Dr. Wang, an expert in industrial safety. "**Regardless** /rɪˈɡɑːrdləs/ (adv. 不管;无论如何) of what the official report says," Dr. Wang told her, "fires don't start themselves. There's always a cause. The question is whether it was accident or **neglect**."

"Could someone have hidden evidence?" Sarah asked.

"It's possible," Dr. Wang said. "But you'll **need** **strength** /streŋθ/ (n. 力量;实力) to pursue this. Companies have **defense** /dɪˈfens/ (n. 防御;辩护) lawyers. They'll **defend** /dɪˈfend/ (v. 防御;辩护) themselves **highly** effectively."

Sarah thought about this. She wasn't just facing a story—she was facing a potential **battle**. **Yet**, she felt driven to continue. This was why she had become a **reporter**: to find truth, **regardless** of obstacles.

That evening, Sarah received an **unknown** /ʌnˈnoʊn/ (adj. 未知的;不出名的) caller. A man's voice said, "If you want to know what really happened, meet me at the old **warehouse** near the factory. Come alone. **Yet** be careful—there are people who don't want this story told."

Sarah's hands shook slightly as she considered this. Meeting an **unknown** person alone could be dangerous. She called her friend Marcus, a photographer who had worked with her on difficult stories before. "I'm **not** going alone," she decided. "Can you come with me as backup?"

Marcus agreed. That night, they drove to the warehouse. The **temperature** had dropped even further, and their breath formed clouds in the cold air. As they approached, a figure stepped from the shadows. It was a young woman, not the man Sarah had expected.

"I'm the one who called," the woman said. "I used a voice app. I couldn't risk being identified." Her name was Lin, and she had been a supervisor at the factory. "What I'm about to tell you requires **strength** to hear. The fire wasn't an accident. Someone **loaded** /loʊdɪd/ (v. 装载;加载[load的过去式]) flammable materials near the heating system **deliberately**."

"Why would someone do that?" Sarah asked, her **reporter**'s instinct focused on getting facts.

"Insurance money," Lin said quietly. "The company was failing. The owner needed a way out. He didn't think anyone would get hurt because the fire was planned for a weekend. But someone came in that day unexpectedly. They barely escaped with their life."

Sarah felt her chest tighten. This was bigger than she had imagined. "Will you testify to this?" she asked.

Lin looked afraid. "I could be **trapped** /træpt/ (v./adj. 困住;陷入困境[trap的过去式]) if I come forward. **Normally**, I wouldn't have the **courage**. But I can't **keep** quiet anymore. A **convicted** /kənˈvɪktɪd/ (adj./v. 被定罪的[convict的过去式]) **person** /ˈpɜːrsn/ could go free while the truth stays hidden."

"We'll **protect** you," Sarah promised, though she knew the challenges ahead. "The truth is our **defense**, and we'll **defend** it together."

Over the next weeks, Sarah worked harder than ever. She verified every fact, found more witnesses, and built a case that couldn't be **dragged** /dræɡd/ (v. 拖;拉[drag的过去式]) down by legal threats. She interviewed experts about how fires **spread**, about **temperature** patterns, about safety regulations. Each fact was another **stroke** /stroʊk/ (n. 中风;划;击) toward completing the picture.

The article Sarah finally published was powerful. It detailed the **theoretical** risks that had been ignored, the **preference** for profit over safety, and the **strength** of those who spoke up **regardless** of personal cost. The **effect** /ɪˈfekt/ (n. 效果;影响) was immediate. The police reopened the investigation. The factory owner was arrested and later **convicted** /kənˈvɪktɪd/ (v. 定罪;判刑[convict]) of multiple crimes.

At the trial, the prosecutor built a strong **defense** of the victims' rights. The factory owner tried to **defend** himself, claiming ignorance, but the evidence was overwhelming. He had **loaded** the situation with risks, and people had been hurt. When the judge announced the verdict, Sarah felt a **bite** /baɪt/ (n./v. 咬;叮) of satisfaction—justice had been served.

After the trial, Lin approached Sarah. "Thank you," she said. "You gave me the **strength** to tell my story. **Normally**, people like me stay silent. **Yet** you showed that one **reporter** with determination can make a difference."

Sarah smiled. "It wasn't just me. It was everyone who had the courage to speak. **Regardless** of the risks, you chose truth. That's real **strength**."

As Sarah walked back to her office, she thought about her journey. She had **encountered** danger, faced **unknown** challenges, and been tested in ways she hadn't expected. The **temperature** of the situation had been **highly** intense. **Yet** she had **defended** the truth, and that **defense** had held.

A young journalism student stopped her on the street. "I read your article," the student said. "It made me want to become a **reporter**. How do you find the **strength** to do this work?"

Sarah thought for a moment. "**Surely**, it's not easy," she said. "You'll **encounter** obstacles and face people who **prefer** you stay quiet. **Yet** remember why you're doing it. Every story matters. Every truth uncovered makes a difference. **Regardless** of how small you feel, your **search** for truth has power."

The student thanked her and walked away with new purpose. Sarah continued to her office, where her editor was waiting. "Ready for the next story?" he asked.

Sarah nodded. "Always. **Yet** this time, let's start with coffee. The **temperature** outside is freezing, and I could use something warm."

They laughed. Sarah had learned that even in serious work, you needed moments of lightness. The **search** for truth was **highly** important, but so was maintaining the **strength** to continue. **Regardless** of what challenges lay ahead, she was ready. She had found her voice, her courage, and her purpose. And that was a story worth living.

## Part 2. 故事翻译 (中文)

记者在进入大楼前又检查了一次手机。那天早上温度急剧下降,莎拉把外套拉紧了。她来这里寻找关于一个困扰这座城市数周的故事的答案。

一场工厂火灾被裁定为意外,但感觉不对劲。莎拉的编辑对官方报告高度怀疑。"去搜索那个区域,"他说。"和人们交谈。找出他们没告诉我们的事。"然而,当莎拉接近被烧毁的建筑时,她感到一阵不确定。

在工厂残余的部分,莎拉遇到了陈,一位前工人。"他们说那天里面的温度达到了危险水平,"陈解释说。"但通常,我们有安全系统。它们应该起作用。"

"你认为发生了什么?"莎拉问,她偏好直截了当,这在她直接的问题中显示出来。

陈犹豫了。"我不确定,但我认为这个故事还有更多内容。肯定有人知道真相。"

接下来的几天,莎拉继续她的搜索。她访问了一些工厂工人接受安全培训的校园。她与了解理论风险的工程师交谈。每次遭遇都带来了新的拼图碎片,但没有确定的东西。

然后莎拉有了一个重要的遭遇。在一家咖啡店,她遇到了王医生,一位工业安全专家。"无论官方报告怎么说,"王医生告诉她,"火灾不会自己开始。总有一个原因。问题是它是意外还是疏忽。"

"有人可能隐藏了证据吗?"莎拉问。

"有可能,"王医生说。"但你需要力量来追查这个。公司有辩护律师。他们会非常有效地为自己辩护。"

莎拉考虑着这个问题。她不仅面对一个故事——她面对的是一场潜在的战斗。然而,她感到有动力继续。这就是她成为记者的原因:寻找真相,无论有什么障碍。

那天晚上,莎拉接到一个未知来电者的电话。一个男人的声音说:"如果你想知道真正发生了什么,在工厂附近的旧仓库见我。一个人来。但要小心——有些人不想让这个故事被讲出来。"

莎拉在考虑这个问题时手微微发抖。单独见一个未知的人可能很危险。她打电话给她的朋友马库斯,一位曾与她一起处理困难故事的摄影师。"我不打算一个人去,"她决定。"你能作为后援和我一起来吗?"

马库斯同意了。那天晚上,他们开车去仓库。温度进一步下降,他们的呼吸在寒冷的空气中形成云雾。当他们接近时,一个身影从阴影中走出。是一位年轻女士,不是莎拉预期的男人。

"我是打电话的人,"那位女士说。"我用了语音应用。我不能冒被识别的风险。"她的名字叫林,曾是工厂的主管。"我将要告诉你的需要力量来听。火灾不是意外。有人故意在加热系统附近装载了易燃材料。"

"为什么有人会这样做?"莎拉问,她的记者本能专注于获取事实。

"保险金,"林平静地说。"公司正在倒闭。老板需要一条出路。他认为不会有人受伤,因为火灾计划在周末。但那天有人意外进来了。他们几乎无法逃生。"

莎拉感到胸口发紧。这比她想象的要大。"你会作证吗?"她问。

林看起来很害怕。"如果我站出来,我可能会陷入困境。通常,我不会有这样的勇气。但我不能再保持沉默了。一个有罪的人可能逍遥法外,而真相却被隐藏。"

"我们会保护你,"莎拉承诺,尽管她知道前面的挑战。"真相是我们的防御,我们将一起捍卫它。"

接下来的几周,莎拉比以往任何时候都更加努力。她核实每个事实,找到更多证人,建立了一个不能被法律威胁拖垮的案例。她采访了关于火灾如何蔓延、温度模式、安全法规的专家。每个事实都是完成整幅图画的又一笔。

莎拉最终发表的文章很有力量。它详细描述了被忽视的理论风险、利润优先于安全的偏好,以及那些无论个人代价如何都敢于发声的人的力量。效果是立竿见影的。警方重新调查。工厂老板被逮捕,后来被定罪多项罪行。

在审判中,检察官为受害者的权利建立了强大的辩护。工厂老板试图为自己辩护,声称无知,但证据是压倒性的。他装载了风险的情况,人们受伤了。当法官宣布判决时,莎拉感到一阵满足——正义得到了伸张。

审判后,林走近莎拉。"谢谢你,"她说。"你给了我讲述我的故事的力量。通常,像我这样的人保持沉默。然而你表明了一个有决心的记者可以带来改变。"

莎拉微笑。"不只是我。是每个有勇气说话的人。无论风险如何,你选择了真相。那才是真正的力量。"

当莎拉走回办公室时,她想到了她的旅程。她遭遇了危险,面对未知的挑战,以她没有预料到的方式受到考验。情况的温度非常激烈。然而她捍卫了真相,那个防御坚持住了。

一位年轻的新闻系学生在街上拦住她。"我读了你的文章,"学生说。"它让我想成为一名记者。你如何找到力量做这项工作?"

莎拉想了一会儿。"当然,这并不容易,"她说。"你会遇到障碍,面对更愿意你保持安静的人。然而记住你为什么这样做。每个故事都很重要。每个揭开的真相都会带来改变。无论你感觉自己多么渺小,你对真相的搜索都有力量。"

学生感谢她,带着新的目标走开了。莎拉继续去她的办公室,她的编辑在等着。"准备好下一个故事了吗?"他问。

莎拉点点头。"随时。但这次,让我们从咖啡开始。外面的温度在冰点,我需要一些温暖的东西。"

他们笑了。莎拉学到了即使在严肃的工作中,你也需要轻松的时刻。寻找真相高度重要,但保持继续的力量也同样重要。无论前方有什么挑战,她都准备好了。她找到了自己的声音、勇气和目标。那是一个值得活着的故事。

## Part 4. 重点句讲解

### 句子 1
**原句:** "Yet, as Sarah approached the burned building, she felt a wave of uncertainty."

**分析:** "Yet" 作为连词放在句首,表示转折。"as" 引导时间状语从句,表示"当...时候"。"felt a wave of" 是一个生动的比喻,将抽象的情感比作具体的波浪。

**亮点:** 使用 "wave" 这个隐喻让抽象的不确定感变得可感知,是优秀的感官写作。

---

### 句子 2
**原句:** "Regardless of what the official report says, fires don't start themselves."

**分析:** "Regardless of" 引导让步状语,表示"不管...如何"。主句使用简单直接的语言陈述事实,增强说服力。

**亮点:** 这种"不管...但是..."的结构在论证时非常有力,先承认可能的反对意见,再提出自己的观点。

---

### 句子 3
**原句:** "She wasn't just facing a story—she was facing a potential battle."

**分析:** 使用 "not just...but..." 的对比结构(这里用破折号代替 but),将两个层次的挑战并列,后者更加严重。两个 "facing" 的重复增强节奏感。

**亮点:** 破折号的使用创造戏剧性停顿,让读者意识到问题的严重性升级。

---

### 句子 4
**原句:** "Regardless of how small you feel, your search for truth has power."

**分析:** "Regardless of" 再次出现,但这次引导的是 "how + 形容词" 的从句,表示程度让步。主句简洁有力,给出鼓励性结论。

**亮点:** 这句话作为文章的主题句之一,将个人渺小感和真相的力量形成对比,鼓舞人心。

## Part 5. 语法聚焦 (Grammar Focus)

### 聚焦点 1: Regardless of 的让步用法

**原句:** "Regardless of what the official report says, fires don't start themselves."

**聚焦:** regardless of 引导让步状语

**用法说明:** 
"Regardless of" 意为"不管、无论",引导让步状语,后面可以接名词、代词或从句。它比 "despite" 和 "in spite of" 语气更强,强调"完全不考虑"。

**结构:**
- Regardless of + 名词: Regardless of the cost
- Regardless of + 从句: Regardless of what happens
- Regardless of + 疑问词从句: Regardless of how hard it is

**类似表达:**
- Despite / In spite of (后接名词)
- No matter what / how / who (后接从句)

**可替换句:** "No matter what the official report says, fires don't start themselves."

---

### 聚焦点 2: Yet 作为转折连词

**原句:** "Yet, as Sarah approached the burned building, she felt a wave of uncertainty."

**聚焦:** yet 的多种用法

**用法说明:**
"Yet" 有多种用法:
1. 作副词,表示"还、仍然"(用于否定句和疑问句): I haven't finished yet.
2. 作连词,表示"但是、然而"(类似 but,但更正式): It's small, yet powerful.
3. 作连词放句首,后接逗号: Yet, she continued.

**与 but 的区别:**
- Yet 更正式,常用于书面语
- Yet 可以放句首,but 一般不单独放句首
- Yet 强调对比或意外

**可替换句:** "However, as Sarah approached the burned building, she felt a wave of uncertainty."
